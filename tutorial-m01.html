Analogue Experience | Decollage

Introduction

In this tutorial, we’ll explore a few simple techniques to create a unique decollage.

Decollage is a process where, after layering and gluing materials, we intentionally remove parts of what we’ve added. Some sections—especially those that have dried—stay in place, while others tear away. This creates textured surfaces with rough edges and interesting layers.

Tools & Materials

  • Wood Art Panel (or heavy cardboard)

  • Acrylic Paints

  • Collage Materials (like your previous experiments)

  • Matte Medium

  • 100% Pure Acetone

1

Begin by covering your wood panel with a layer of acrylic paint. Don’t worry about making it even — the texture adds character. Once it’s dry, add a generous amount of paint in a darker or lighter shade to build contrast, and spread it across the surface using the edge of a used gift card. Let it dry completely.

2

Print your desired texture onto draft bond paper using a laser printer. Place the print face-down on your painted background, with the white side facing up. Gently apply pure acetone to the back of the paper using a tissue or brush, pressing evenly until the image transfers onto the surface.

3

Tear or cut pieces of printed material—such as newspapers, graph paper, and old drafts—and layer them freely over the surface. Use matte medium to glue them down. Before the glued layers are completely dry, begin gently removing parts of the paper. The areas that have dried will stay in place, while the semi-wet parts will lift off, creating torn edges and interesting distressed textures.

4

Add more collage materials or patches of color based on your artistic taste. This is your chance to bring balance, contrast, or highlight specific areas. Want the colors to show up softly? Here’s a golden tip: place the printed or painted side face-down for a more subtle, diffused effect. You can also layer transparent or tissue-like papers to let underlying textures peek through. Let your eye guide you — this final step is where the artwork becomes yours.

5

Add your final touches to bring everything together. In this example, I used pastel to introduce texture and soft color accents, but feel free to experiment with whatever materials you like — pencil, ink, or even a touch of more paint. This last layer is your opportunity to unify the piece and add personal detail. Let your instincts guide you.
